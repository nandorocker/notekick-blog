  <description/>
  <content:encoded><![CDATA[Taking notes can be a mess. During my studies, I always had a desk full of books, paper notes, notebooks, and a laptop full of windows – Word, PDFs, websites, multiple note taking applications.

Believe me, I tried every note taking app out there and always ended up with the same problem: clutter. Switching between apps, remembering where you saved a note or reference, choosing the best one for each task. That's when the idea for Notekick was born.

Since the site went live in June, we've been busy bees, working hard at improving the service. We've received an overwhelmingly positive response, and lots of important feedback (from users and ourselves), which we're using to make the app as useful to you as possible.
<h2>New Features Coming Soon</h2>
In the next coming weeks we'll be rolling out an improved version of Notekick. Here are some features you can expect.
<h3>Workspaces</h3>
<a href="http://notekick.files.wordpress.com/2013/11/blog-_0002_workspaces.png"><img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-78" alt="Workspaces" src="http://notekick.files.wordpress.com/2013/11/blog-_0002_workspaces.png" width="670" height="314" /></a>

Group your notes according to your needs. Just create (or choose) your notes, lay them out and that's it! Each workspace has a name, and can be quickly accessed by a single click.
<h3>Columns</h3>
<img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-77" style="font-size:11.48px;line-height:1.5;" alt="Columns" src="http://notekick.files.wordpress.com/2013/11/blog-_0001_columns.png" width="670" height="314" />

Want your notes side-by-side? Three in a row? One long one, one thin? Simply choose from a number of columns and your notes will know where to go.
<h3>All Notes</h3>
<h3><img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-76" style="font-size:11.48px;line-height:1.5;" alt="All Notes" src="http://notekick.files.wordpress.com/2013/11/blog-_0000_all-notes.png" width="670" height="314" /></h3>
Browse every note you've ever written, and add them to your Workspace. You can sort alphabetically or by date, and filter by keyword.

We're still very much in beta and would love to hear your feedback. Or if you a have an idea you'd like to see incorporated into NoteKick, just email us directly at <a href="mailto:info@notekick.com">info@notekick.com</a>.  We'll do our best to respond back to you!

Haven't signed up for Notekick yet? Sign up <span style="color:#ff6600;"><strong><a href="http://www.notekick.com" target="_blank"><span style="color:#ff6600;">here!</span></a></strong></span>]]></content:encoded>

If you’ve ever seen National Lampoon’s Van Wilder, you’ll know it’s full of priceless, if not ironic, truths that can apply to every aspect of life. Yes, even note taking. Here are five tips from the movie that struck me as particularly “note” worthy.
<h2></h2>
<h2></h2>
<h2> 1. “Write that down.”</h2>
<p dir="ltr">Van’s catch phrase really makes the point. The simple act of writing something down fires your three primary learning engines: auditory, kinesthetic and visual.  First, when you actively listen to a lecture. Then, with the typity-type from taking digital notes.  And lastly, by looking at and reviewing the notes you’ve taken. Each step reinforces and compounds your brain’s ability to remember information long-term.</p>

<h2> 2. “Sometimes in life, you need to recognize a poor investment and just cut your losses.”</h2>
<p dir="ltr">The internet has a lot of information, too much information, and it’s not all helpful or even true. So, when doing online research, the ability to identify time sucks, rabbit holes and unreliable sources can save your sanity.  This is where a multi-note environment comes in handy. Have several notes open in front of you at the same time and begin to group them into relevant clusters. A hierarchy will soon develop and you’ll have a better idea of how to devote your energy and manage your study time.</p>

<h2> 3. “If you think about the future too much, you forget about the present.”</h2>
<p dir="ltr">People often complain that today’s students go to college for a degree, not an education. I totally admit to being one of these students, at least on a micro level. When taking notes, I became overly focused on identifying future test answers that often missed the point of the lecture. My Pathology teacher had a radical suggestion for staying in the moment: don’t take notes at all. Well, not until after the lecture. This forced me to use my active listening skills. The process of summarizing what I’d heard later helped me prioritize and identify what I needed study in more depth.</p>

<h2> 4. “Are you stalking me? Because that would be super.”</h2>
<p dir="ltr">I’m sure that Van Wilder would have tweeted this. These days, people pass notes online. And why not? Sharing notes on Facebook, Twitter, or right here on the Public Workspace, is a great way to connect with like minds and begin insightful conversations. Plus, copying public notes is more acceptable significantly less creepy than hovering over the class brainiac.</p>

<h2> 5. “Don’t take life too seriously, you’ll never get out alive.”</h2>
<p dir="ltr">If there’s anything to take away from watching this movie, it probably should be this. When taking notes don’t forget to take time to enjoy what you’re learning. Stressing out isn’t gonna make you smarter or happier, and it definitely won’t help you live longer.</p>

  <link>http://blog.notekick.com/2013/11/08/using-cornell-notes-digitally/</link>

  <description/>
  <content:encoded><![CDATA[<p dir="ltr">I take a lot of notes.  Class, work, diy projects, art projects, even when just planning dinner - I take pages of notes. One method for wrangling them all that works well for me is the good-old Cornell Notes method. You may be familiar with it. It’s been the academic standard for decades mostly because it is set up so you can see all your main points, at once, on one sheet of paper. Ah, there’s the rub. Who takes notes on paper anymore? Adapting Cornell Note to online note taking is easy on Notekick. The multi-note Workspace naturally lends itself to the multi-column format.</p>

<h2>Setting up Worksheets with the Cornell Notes Method</h2>
[caption id="attachment_99" align="alignleft" width="180"]<a href="http://notekick.files.wordpress.com/2013/11/images-1.jpg"><img class=" wp-image-99   " alt="Screen shot of workspace set up with notes in this format" src="http://notekick.files.wordpress.com/2013/11/images-1.jpg" width="180" height="180" /></a> Screen shot of workspace set up with notes in this format[/caption]
<ul>
<ul>
	<li><span style="font-size:11.48px;line-height:1.5;">Simply create a Workspace and divide it into two columns with an optional section on the bottom.</span></li>
	<li><span style="font-size:11.48px;line-height:1.5;">RIGHT COLUMN: Make this column about twice as wide as the left, This is where you’ll take down as much information as possible during a lecture or reading. It’s okay to be messy here, so don’t waste time editing. Just let it flow.</span></li>
	<li><span style="font-size:11.48px;line-height:1.5;">RIGHT COLUMN: As big ideas present themselves, put them in separate notes in this narrower column on your Workspace. This is also a good place for key vocab terms, slide bullet points and the stuff that’s usually bolded or italicized in textbooks.</span></li>
	<li><span style="font-size:11.48px;line-height:1.5;">BOTTOM SECTION (optional): Traditionally this is where you’d put a brief post note-taking summary but it’s not always necessary. An alternative is to add tags to your notes. This makes finding the exact ones you need quick and easy.</span></li>
</ul>
</ul>
<p dir="ltr">These steps make up the first R in Cornell’s suggested Q3Rs study and retain process: Record, Question, Recite, Reflect, Review. The remaining parts work pretty much the same on paper as they do on your laptop or tablet. Followed correctly, they’re shown to be effective in reducing the stress that comes with working with multiple notes and to increase information retention.</p>
